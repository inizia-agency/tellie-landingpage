Executive Summary

Tellie is an empathetic technology platform designed specifically for older adults. It begins as a voice-first AI companion and is intended to evolve into a broader healthy-aging and digital-inclusion platform.

The core insight behind Tellie is simple: many older adults do not struggle because technology is unavailable to them, but because most technology is not designed for them. Interfaces are often complex, fast, cognitively demanding, and built for digitally native users. At the same time, aging populations across Australia, the United States, the United Kingdom, Canada, and New Zealand are facing rising loneliness, social isolation, and growing exclusion from an increasingly digital society.

Tellie addresses this by designing around a familiar behavior older adults already know: making a phone call. Rather than forcing seniors to learn a new interaction model, Tellie uses a voice-first, elder-driven interface that feels more like calling someone than using software. The product is built to be warm, simple, patient, and relationship-oriented.

Tellie's wedge is companionship. Its long-term platform opportunity is much larger: digital inclusion, healthy aging, family reassurance, community participation, and wellbeing support for older adults.

Tellie will initially launch with a hybrid B2C and B2B model:

  • B2C subscriptions for older adults and adult children
  • B2B distribution through councils, community centers, home care providers, and aged-care facilities

The long-term investment thesis is that Tellie can become the most trusted technology experience for older adults by combining senior-first design, longitudinal relationship memory, voice interaction, and institutional distribution.

Founder Story

Tellie was born from personal experience with loneliness and from observing the emotional impact of isolation among older adults, including within the founder's own family. The founder's experience as an immigrant reinforced the emotional cost of disconnection and informed the company's focus on connection, belonging, and human-centered technology.

Company History

Origins

Tellie was created from a simple belief: technology should serve humans, not the other way around. From the beginning, the company was shaped by the observation that while technology was becoming more powerful, many older adults were being left further behind by products that were not designed for their needs, confidence levels, or lived realities.

Product before funding

Tellie's early operating model was built around a two-founder structure in which product development and fundraising/growth responsibilities were intended to run in parallel. In practice, external capital for marketing and user acquisition did not materialize at the pace originally expected.

Rather than waiting for funding to begin execution, the company chose to continue building. That decision resulted in a working product being developed before institutional investment, significantly reducing technical execution risk.

Today, Tellie has progressed beyond concept stage and already includes:

  • a live mobile product across iOS and Android
  • real-time voice and chat experiences
  • memory and personalization systems
  • subscription infrastructure
  • organizational activation flows
  • usage and entitlement controls

Enterprise validation and strategic setback

During development, Tellie identified a significant B2B opportunity with a large health insurer in Latin America. The proposed collaboration explored how Tellie's conversational and voice capabilities could support a preventive-health and voice-analysis protocol.

The commercial pathway discussed included:

  • an initial validation cohort of approximately 10,000 users
  • a potential broader rollout to approximately 1.4 million insured members
  • insurer-backed infrastructure and development support for the protocol integration

Although the opportunity did not ultimately proceed because the insurer entered an M&A process and strategic priorities changed, the engagement provided meaningful validation that a large enterprise stakeholder saw value in Tellie's platform beyond companionship alone.

Problem

The world is aging

Developed nations are aging rapidly. Australia, the United States, the United Kingdom, Canada, and New Zealand all have growing 65+ populations, increasing strain on healthcare systems, care networks, and community support structures.

Loneliness and social isolation are serious public-health issues

Research cited in the source register indicates:

  • WHO reports that 1 in 6 people globally experience loneliness
  • WHO links loneliness and social isolation to approximately 871,000 deaths annually
  • The U.S. Surgeon General associates social disconnection with depression, anxiety, dementia, stroke, cardiovascular disease, and premature mortality
  • Older adults are disproportionately vulnerable because of retirement, loss of partners, reduced mobility, shrinking social circles, and family dispersion

Digital exclusion amplifies the problem

The opportunity is not only to reduce loneliness. It is to reduce digital exclusion, which affects:

  • social participation
  • confidence
  • independence
  • access to services
  • family connection
  • local engagement
  • healthy aging

Many seniors do have access to devices, but struggle with:

  • complex interfaces
  • lack of confidence
  • fear of making mistakes
  • products designed for younger users
  • inaccessible or cognitively demanding workflows

Why existing solutions fall short

General-purpose AI

Products like ChatGPT, Gemini, and Claude are powerful but not optimized for older adults. They are broad, feature-heavy, and not built around emotional safety, simplicity, or senior-specific interaction design.

Human companion services

Human support is valuable but expensive, geographically limited, and difficult to scale.

Companion robots

Products such as ElliQ show that the market exists, but hardware introduces adoption friction, setup complexity, and higher acquisition cost.

Solution

Tellie is a voice-first AI companion designed specifically for older adults. It is not intended to replace human relationships. It is intended to multiply moments of connection and provide support when family, carers, or community are not immediately available.

The product's design thesis is that older adults may not be comfortable navigating advanced software, but most are comfortable making a phone call. Tellie therefore presents itself through a familiar call-like experience rather than a complex AI workspace.

Product And How It Works

Product philosophy

Tellie is built around one central product question:

What can an older adult already do comfortably?

The answer is: call.

Tellie is designed to feel like a phone call rather than a traditional AI tool. The product reduces adoption friction by matching an existing user habit.

Elder-driven interface

Tellie's user experience is intentionally optimized for older adults:

  • voice-first interaction
  • minimal screen complexity
  • low cognitive load
  • familiar communication patterns
  • slower, gentler pacing
  • emotional comfort
  • large, simple interaction cues

The goal is not to expose the full power of AI. The goal is to make technology disappear into a familiar and dignified interaction.

Market Opportunity

Why now

Three trends support the market opportunity:

  1. Aging populations are expanding across core English-speaking markets.
  2. Loneliness and social isolation are increasingly treated as public-health and economic issues.
  3. Conversational AI is now mature enough to support scalable, natural dialogue at consumer level.

Demographic shift: the inversion of the population pyramid

One of the most important macro trends underpinning Tellie is population aging.

Historically, many countries resembled a population pyramid, with large younger populations supporting relatively smaller older populations. Across developed economies, that structure is shifting because of declining birth rates and increasing life expectancy. The result is an increasingly top-heavy or inverted age profile, with more older adults and proportionally fewer younger people.

This is not a temporary anomaly. It is one of the most predictable long-term demographic changes underway.

According to the Australian Institute of Health and Welfare, older Australians already represented more than one in six Australians in 2020, and people aged 65 and over are projected to represent 21% to 23% of the population by 2066. The Australian Government's Intergenerational Report identifies population aging as one of the major structural forces shaping the country's future economy and care burden.

In the United States, the U.S. Census Bureau reported that the 65+ population reached 61.2 million in 2024, representing 18.0% of the population. Across other core Tellie markets including the United Kingdom, Canada, and New Zealand, official statistics show the same directional trend: the senior share of the population continues to rise.

At global level, the United Nations has projected that population aging will continue accelerating over coming decades, fundamentally reshaping healthcare demand, family structures, social support systems, and the design requirements for consumer technology.

Why mobile first, not robotics

Several companies in the healthy-aging and companionship space are investing in robotics. Tellie believes robotics may become an important part of the future, but that the right initial market strategy is mobile-first.

Today's seniors are not tomorrow's seniors

For the next 10 to 15 years, a large share of older adults will still be less digitally native than the generations that follow them. Many continue to experience friction with:

  • new interfaces
  • setup-heavy devices
  • product onboarding
  • digital confidence

At the same time, smartphones have already achieved much broader familiarity than dedicated robots or new hardware categories.

Trust comes before hardware

Tellie's thesis is that companionship is fundamentally a trust problem before it is a hardware problem.

Older adults are unlikely to adopt unfamiliar devices or robotic companions at scale unless trust has already been established. By delivering companionship through devices seniors or their families already understand, Tellie can focus first on what matters most:

  • trust
  • habit
  • emotional connection
  • longitudinal relationship building

Once those are established, new form factors become easier to introduce.

Building for the current wave, preparing for the next

Tellie's immediate opportunity is today's older adult population. By serving this generation now, the company aims to earn trust from the hardest cohort to win: seniors who are most likely to need digital simplicity and most likely to distrust unfamiliar technology.

That creates long-term strategic value in the form of:

  • user trust
  • family trust
  • behavioral and relationship data
  • brand recognition
  • channel partnerships

As the next generation of older adults ages into the market, digital literacy will likely be materially higher. Many of those future seniors will already have used smartphones, voice interfaces, AI tools, and other connected technologies for decades. At that point, Tellie expects additional expansion opportunities across:

  • dedicated companion devices
  • smart home integrations
  • wearables
  • robotics

Mobile-first operating advantages

Every robotics company must solve:

  • hardware
  • manufacturing
  • distribution
  • companionship
  • trust

Tellie only needs to solve:

  • companionship
  • trust

That makes the mobile-first strategy a competitive advantage, not a limitation. It enables:

  • lower capital requirements
  • faster iteration cycles
  • faster distribution
  • quicker product-market fit validation
  • optionality for future hardware expansion

Mobile is not Tellie's final form factor. Mobile is Tellie's fastest path to becoming the trusted relationship layer for older adults.

Financial Model

Financial model purpose

Tellie's financial model needs to answer four investor questions:

  1. Can Tellie acquire users efficiently?
  2. Can Tellie retain users long enough to recover acquisition cost?
  3. Can Tellie deliver AI-powered companionship with healthy margins?
  4. Can Tellie expand from B2C into more scalable B2B revenue?

Key modelling assumptions

  • AI stack uses OpenAI chat and realtime voice products
  • hosting baseline assumption: USD $20/month initially
  • data stack uses Google NoSQL infrastructure
  • app distribution uses Apple App Store and Google Play
  • conservative app store fee case: 30%
  • optimized app store fee case: 15%
  • free users must be tightly usage-capped
  • premium usage must be governed by entitlement and cost controls

Direct cost drivers

AI costs

Primary direct costs:

  • text input tokens
  • text output tokens
  • realtime voice input tokens
  • realtime voice output tokens
  • transcription or audio-related processing

App store commissions

  • Apple and Google meaningfully affect consumer gross margin

Infrastructure

  • hosting
  • database reads/writes/storage
  • bandwidth

Operating support

  • customer support
  • accounting
  • company compliance
  • insurance
  • legal and data/privacy costs as the business matures

App store and platform baseline assumptions

  • Apple Developer Program: USD $99/year
  • Google Play Console: USD $25 one-time registration
  • Apple small business program: eligible developers may pay 15% instead of 30%
  • Google Play states most developers subject to service fees qualify for 15% or less

Database assumptions

Firestore-style usage is expected to be negligible at early stage relative to AI costs. The database becomes more material only at larger scale or with very chatty read/write patterns.

Assumption to validate: early-stage database cost remains under roughly USD $50/month until usage grows materially.

Risks And Mitigation

User adoption risk

Risk:

  • seniors may not adopt or repeat usage at expected levels

Mitigation:

  • community-first pilots
  • phone-call-style onboarding
  • heavy usability iteration

Retention risk

Risk:

  • novelty may not convert into durable habit

Mitigation:

  • optimize for weekly meaningful conversations
  • measure emotional value, not just activity

AI cost risk

Risk:

  • realtime voice usage could erode margins

Mitigation:

  • tiered usage controls
  • model optimization
  • entitlement limits
  • enterprise bundles

Competitive/platform risk

Risk:

  • general AI platforms could add senior-friendly features

Mitigation:

  • build distribution, trust, senior-specific UX, and longitudinal relationship data

Regulatory risk

Risk:

  • the product could drift into regulated health territory

Mitigation:

  • remain in companionship and wellbeing framing until a formal regulatory strategy is established

B2B sales cycle risk

Risk:

  • institutional contracts may be slow

Mitigation:

  • start with low-friction pilots through community and council channels
